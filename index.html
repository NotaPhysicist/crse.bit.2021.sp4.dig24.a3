What we believe.

We believe in the possibility of a great wisdom — and if it exists, that it is ours to discover — embedded in the language of our forebears.

How it works.

Our work begins and ends in the dictionary. The dictionary is our scene of investigation.

This presentation is about one English word, happiness. It could be about any word but we've arbitrarily chosen happiness for this experimental presentation.

Words are defined with other words. We use a simple technique of recognising and eliminating the circularity of dictionary definitions in which a word attempts define itself in terms of itself, thereby cutting through to the core of the word.

Confused?

We will walk you through it, one step at a time.

Please keep scrolling to see the rest of the presentation.

Happiness.

It's a word we're likely to see, hear or use every day, if only in our thoughts.

We strive for it, suffer for the lack of it, and die with the feeling we never had enough of it.

But the word is not the thing.

What thing does happiness describe?

Definition.

The dictionary defines happiness as the state of being happy.

But that's not very helpful, is it?

We still don't know what happy is.

So let's dig a little deeper.

Happy.

The dictionary defines happy as feeling or showing pleasure or contentment. As with most words, it has other idiomatic meanings, but we can take this is the core meaning of the word.

This gets us a step closer to the meaning of happiness.

Next.

We'll look at feeling, showing, pleasure and contentment, each in turn.

We will find that pleasure and contentment attempt to define themselves with the word we're trying to define.

This is circularity, and we will need to find a way around it.
